Critical Reflective Journal – Week 10

This week whilst working on our project our team encountered a lot of trouble trying to get additional information from the ALA API. We wanted to pull as much data as we could from APIs. However, upon trying to access more detailed information via an API call, we ran into CORS errors and authentication issues. A lot of it due to poor documentation. We spent a long time troubleshooting the issue to no avail. This was extremely frustrating spending a lot of time troubleshooting an issue with no progress.

One of our team members emailed ALA about the issues we were facing. We then managed to get clarity on what we needed to perform a successful call for the information we desired. It was great action from the team to seek additional information from an external source. In this case we sought help early saving further stress and allowing development of other features of the application.

Overall MVP Reflection:

Working towards our MVP was a challenging and rewarding experience. Our MVP can be divided into three main sections. Developing our designs, developing the MVP and presentation preparations.

This first step in working towards our MVP was turning our sketches into high-fidelity designs in Figma. The first step that was extremely helpful was running more detailed prototyping sessions. I go into more detail about this in CRJ week 7. Once we had completed our paper prototyping session and gained feedback; we had a bit more direction in completing our high-fidelity prototype in Figma. All team members contributed to the design process. However, this is not my strongest point and I struggled a bit with developing the Figma design. This is where help from my team members really came in. My main contributions during this high-fidelity prototyping phase was picking a colour scheme, consolidating inspiration from existing sources to supplement the design as well as mapping interactions using Figma’s prototyping tools. Initially the team struggled to gain momentum in developing our design, but it was a priority to get it right and a take our time with it, which was frustrating at first. However, as we gained more clarity with our design things became easier. This stage was also a learning moment for me as even though I did not get into the nitty gritty of the design work, I saw how I could be valuable in other areas like consolidating inspiration, providing feedback, analysing user testing and making a functioning Figma prototype. I was initially worried that I wouldn’t be able to contribute as much in this stage.

After our designs were finalised in Figma and we completed a bit of user testing with the Figma prototype we were then ready to start development of our MVP. First we delegated rolls for what each member should complete. And from there the first challenge was deciding what platform we would use to develop our MVP. We eventually settled on CodeSandbox and online IDE that allows synchronous web development. This was a good solution for our group as we could develop in real-time. I was given the primary responsibility of developing the sound match game. I began by making sure I could obtain animal sounds from the Atlas of Living (ALA) API. This took me some time for two reasons, the first being poor time management and the second being the time it took me to get familiar with the ALA API. However, once I was familiar with the API I was able to make a fetch request to obtain animal sounds.

My next step was to replicate the design of the sound match programmatically. I struggled with CSS styling initially as it had been a while since I had done web development. This is where my team was valuable in providing support in refreshing me on ways to organise components using CSS as this was a weak spot for me. I was able to replicate the design we had created in Figma as well as implement sound playing when the user clicks the speaker button. However, I was not able to start implementing the actual game functionality.

Finally, once our team had completed our planned development for the MVP we then began preparations for our presentation. Since I completed the interaction plan in the previous presentation I was responsible for presenting how our MVP adhered to the interaction plan and where we could improve. I determined that improvements are needed in orientation, visibility, feedback, error prevention, and language to align more with our interaction plan. This includes ensuring compatibility across different device sizes and orientations through testing, improving visibility by refining colour schemes, fonts and animations, as well as testing edge cases in our games to prevent errors.

Overall, the process of working towards the MVP was both challenging and rewarding. The initial phase of turning sketches into high-fidelity designs in Figma was a key moment for the team. I personally found this stage difficult, as design is not my strongest area, but I was able to contribute in my own way. Even though I was able to make progress I was unable to fully implement the game functionality by the MVP deadline. This experience showed the importance of time management and seeking help. The team helping each other through areas of struggle, was a key factor in overcoming completing the MVP.

The biggest lesson from the development phase was that familiarizing myself with new tools or APIs early in the process is crucial. By addressing these issues earlier, I could have avoided some of the time management problems I faced. Additionally, refreshing skills that may not be used often, such as CSS styling, beforehand would have made the development process smoother.
